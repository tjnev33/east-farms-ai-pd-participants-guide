AI in the K–4 Classroom

60 Minutes  ·  Farmington Public Schools

The biggest risk isn't "AI disruption" — it's believing that what we do now is all there is. Our job today is to notice the unseen opportunities forming and ask: What new thing is this making possible?

Micro-Experiment
Three Phases, One Experience
You're about to have a personalized conversation with AI, build something real with Gemini's tools, and see what your colleagues create. Each phase builds on the last.

Instead of reading through the district's AI guidance as a document, you're going to have a conversation with AI about your teaching. Gemini will interview you — it'll ask you a few questions about who you are, what's on your plate, and where your students could benefit from more access. Then it'll give you a personalized recommendation for something you could try, including which Gemini tool to use and a starter prompt to get going.

To get started: Open Gemini from your Google apps menu (the waffle). Select Pro (Gemini 2.5 Pro) from the model selector. Then follow the steps below.

Step 2: Paste the AI Interview prompt

After sharing the link, copy and paste the prompt below into the same Gemini chat and send it.

AI Interview Prompt
You are a supportive, thoughtful AI coach helping a K–4 teacher discover how AI could support their classroom practice and increase access for all learners. You have read the FPS AI Use Guide I shared and understand the district's approved platforms, expectations, and approach. You are also familiar with the CAST Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework and its three principles: multiple means of engagement, representation, and action & expression. Conduct a short interview — no more than 3 questions, asked one at a time. Wait for my response before asking the next question. Question 1: Ask what grade I teach and what's one thing about my teaching I'm most proud of or energized by right now. Question 2: Ask about a moment recently where I noticed a student (or group of students) struggling to access the content, express their understanding, or stay engaged — and what I tried. Question 3: Based on what you've heard, share a brief observation connecting my strengths to the access challenge I described. Frame this through a specific UDL principle (engagement, representation, or action & expression). Then ask whether I'd like an AI recommendation focused in that direction, or if there's something else on my mind I'd rather explore. After my response to Question 3, provide a clear, specific recommendation that includes: 1. Which specific Gemini tool to use (Canvas, Create Image, Guided Learning, or Create Music) and why it fits 2. How this connects to the UDL principle you identified — be specific about which UDL checkpoint or guideline it supports 3. A concrete example of what the finished product could look like in my classroom 4. A ready-to-use starter prompt I can paste directly into a new Gemini chat with that tool selected Keep the tone warm, practical, and specific to what I've shared. The recommendation should feel like it was designed for me, not pulled from a menu. Do not recommend activities where students independently interact with AI — focus on teacher-driven uses that benefit students.

Now make it real. Open a new Gemini chat, select the model your recommendation suggested, choose the tool from the Tools menu, and paste your starter prompt. You have about 17 minutes to create something you could actually use.

If your interview recommended one tool but you want to try a different one, go for it. The recommendation is a starting point, not an assignment. The goal is that you build something — not that you follow a specific path.

Tool Quick Reference

Canvas
Lesson plans, activities, graphic organizers, rubrics, parent letters — anything you'd want to edit and refine collaboratively with AI
Example: A tiered vocabulary activity for 3rd grade science with built-in scaffolds for emerging readers
UDL: Representation UDL: Action & Expression
Create Image
Visuals for lessons, anchor charts, classroom decor, student-facing materials that remove text-based barriers
Example: An illustrated step-by-step visual schedule for a morning routine
UDL: Representation
Guided Learning
Learning a new concept yourself, exploring a topic before teaching it, building background knowledge on UDL strategies
Example: Understanding how to apply the UDL checkpoint "optimize relevance, value, and authenticity" in a 2nd grade math unit
UDL: Engagement
Create Music
Transition songs, classroom routines, content-specific jingles, calming background audio — multiple means of engagement
Example: A 60-second transition song for clean-up time in kindergarten
UDL: Engagement

Stand up, Chromebook in hand. Walk around the room and look at what other people created. Show each other your screens. You don't need to present — just make your work visible and be curious about what others built.

As you walk, notice: What surprised you? What tool did someone use that you hadn't considered? What UDL connection did someone else make that you want to explore?

After the Session
Your Sandbox
This space is yours to return to whenever you're ready to keep exploring. There's no deadline and no assignment — just three pathways depending on where your curiosity takes you.

You started something during the session — now take it further. Open the Gemini chat where you built your first creation and keep refining, or start a new chat and iterate on the same idea.

Refine what you made. Ask Gemini to adjust the reading level, add more scaffolding, create a version for a different learner profile, or adapt it for a co-teaching model.
Build the next piece. If you created a lesson plan, now create the student-facing materials. If you made an image, create the accompanying activity.
Apply a different UDL lens. Your AI Interview focused on one UDL principle. What would it look like to apply a different principle to the same lesson?

Something you saw during the Show & Stroll sparked a different idea. Follow that curiosity — try a new tool, explore a different UDL principle, or take a completely different approach.

Try a tool you didn't use. If you used Canvas, try Create Image. If you used Create Image, try Guided Learning. See what a different tool unlocks for your practice.
Explore a colleague's idea. Something you saw resonated — now make your own version for your grade level, your content, your students.
Try a new UDL angle. Your session focused on one access challenge. Pick a different moment where students struggle and see what AI suggests.

Plan the lesson. Take what you built and design the classroom experience around it. What does the teacher do? What do students do? Where does the AI-created material fit?
Design for the edges. Think about your highest-need learners. How would you adapt what you created for a student with an IEP? For an emerging multilingual learner? For a student who's already beyond grade level?
Map to UDL. Use Gemini's Guided Learning tool to explore the specific UDL checkpoints your creation supports. Ask it to help you identify which barriers you're removing and which ones remain.
